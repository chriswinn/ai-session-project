Ongoing research tracks

These are open investigations that don't tie to a single purchase decision — they're re-evaluated across sessions as new information surfaces. Each should be updated in future handoffs, not closed out.

Track 1 — Prout Snowgoose 37: for-now boat, or forever boat?

Open question: does the Prout Snowgoose earn a place in the long-term tier, or is it strictly a mid-term stepping stone?

Factors for forever status: proven offshore record across decades of circumnavigations, narrow beam (standard slip fees indefinitely), twin keels for drying out without a cradle, strong value retention, three-cabin layout, bluewater credentials at a working-person price point that doesn't exist anywhere else in the catamaran market.

Factors against: salon headroom is a permanent ergonomic compromise, not just a tolerable one; Prout went bust in 2002 so there is no factory support, parts sourcing is owner-community only; every example is now old enough that structural issues (decks, chainplates) are a real risk regardless of survey; limited upgrade path for modern electrical and propulsion systems compared to newer cat platforms.

Next step: find Prout owners who've completed extended offshore passages in the past 5 years, assess long-term maintainability trajectory vs. newer cat platforms at similar all-in cost. Re-evaluate annually.

Track 2 — Electric propulsion & renewable power systems: forever-boat readiness

Open question: when are all-electric catamaran propulsion systems plus battery + solar + wind generation mature and reliable enough to commit to on a forever boat?

Current read: solar + wind + LiFePO4 house systems are mature now — Victron-class charge controllers, 600–1,200W solar, 100–200Ah LiFePO4 banks, and 400–600W wind generators are reliable and well-documented for offshore use. Hydrogenerators (Watt & Sea and equivalents) add a third dimension on passage. Full electric propulsion is a different question: diesel hybrid remains the offshore standard at accessible price points, and full-electric platforms (Oceanvolt, Torqeedo Deep Blue, Bellmarine, serial-hybrid drop-ins) are still maturing on range, charge times, and serviceability in remote ports.

Readiness criteria — all must be met before committing to a full-electric forever boat:

  • Documented offshore passages of 500+ nm under electric-only propulsion becoming routine in the cruising community, not just demonstration events.
  • Service network for the propulsion system exists in at least two of the major cruising hubs (Caribbean, Mediterranean, Pacific).
  • Replacement battery cost at 10-year mark is known and financeable — not a surprise capital event.
  • At least two production catamaran builders (e.g. Fountaine Pajot, Lagoon, Bali, Leopard) offering electric as a factory option with multi-year fleet data.
  • Solar + wind + hydro generation budget, under realistic cruising conditions, covers both house and propulsion charging with a comfortable margin — not just theoretical sizing.

Reassessment cadence: annually, or on material announcements from the major electric-propulsion vendors or production cat builders.

Implications for tier decisions: until readiness criteria are met, the long-term tier defaults to diesel or diesel-hybrid platforms with strong renewable house systems. Long-term candidates should be preferred if they have an existing electric-conversion or factory-electric pathway, so the platform can evolve as the technology matures rather than forcing a re-purchase.

§5 — Watch items & constraints

  • Don't buy before mapping the insurance qualification path. A vessel that can't be insured for offshore liveaboard use at a reasonable premium is not a viable purchase, regardless of price or condition.
  • Evaluate total-to-slip cost, not sticker price. The Tartan 33 lesson: a low purchase price means nothing if the pre-sail repairs push the all-in past what a better boat would cost. Always compute all-in before comparing options across tiers.
  • Survey is non-negotiable for any purchase, especially older fiberglass and any boat with osmotic blister history.
  • Marina slip terms: Confirm whether the secured slip has an LOA or beam limit before committing to a wide-beam catamaran. Catamarans often need wider slips, and mid-tier Lagoons will not fit standard monohull slips.
  • 20-year hull-age rule is a hard line for most marine loans — it determines which tier a boat falls into regardless of its purchase price.
  • Gemini 105MC known issue: Centerboard design has been criticised for offshore use. Good Chesapeake Bay boat (shallow-water anchorages, protected cruising), not a true bluewater platform — place it in the mid-term tier accordingly, and don't let a good local-cruising experience mask the offshore limits.
  • Lagoon 380 beam warning: ~22ft beam means double slip fees at most marinas. Verify slip compatibility before putting on any active shortlist.

§6 — Project operating core

Project description

Transition from shoreside living to full-time liveaboard on a bluewater-capable sailboat, with a long-term goal of offshore cruising. Near-term: find and purchase a suitable vessel sized to the near-term budget tier, get properly qualified, and move aboard at the secured marina slip. The near and mid-term cruising grounds are the Chesapeake Bay and adjacent coastal waters — shallow, shoaly, and known for short steep summer chop — which favors either shoal-draft hulls (keel-centerboards, twin keels, catamarans with boards up) or heavier-displacement monohulls that ride chop well. Mid and long-term: progress through the boat tiers as savings and credentials allow, toward an eventual offshore-ready platform.

Methodology

Decisions are made in qualification order: safety and insurability first, then seaworthiness, then comfort, then cost. No boat is evaluated as a purchase candidate until the insurance qualification pathway is mapped. Boats are matched to financial tiers by all-in cost (purchase + survey contingency + delivery + immediate must-fix items), not by sticker price. Research is primary-source where possible (owner forums, class associations, surveyor reports) rather than aggregator sites.

Key terminology

  • Bluewater capable: Suitable for offshore passages in open ocean conditions, not just coastal cruising. Minimum standard for the long-term tier; optional for near and mid-term tiers where a coastal-focused boat can make sense as a stepping stone.
  • Liveaboard: Living aboard full-time, not just weekend use. Insurance and marina requirements differ significantly from part-time use.
  • All-in cost: Purchase price + survey + delivery + any immediate pre-sail must-fix items. The number that actually determines which tier a boat falls into.
  • Near-term tier: Cash-only, pre-2006 hulls, expect deferred maintenance. See §3 shortlist.
  • Mid-term tier: Cash quality or financing-eligible hulls. Catamarans first become realistic here. See §3 shortlist.
  • Long-term tier: Financing-eligible newer production or premium classics. Later-career acquisition. See §3 shortlist.

Decision rules

  • If a boat can't be insured for offshore liveaboard use, it's off the list regardless of other merits.
  • If a boat's all-in cost (purchase + survey + delivery + pre-sail must-fixes) exceeds the ceiling of a higher tier, re-evaluate against that higher tier's shortlist instead.
  • When evaluating a new boat candidate, always check: age, builder reputation, known class issues, osmotic blister history, rig condition, engine hours.
  • Budget estimates should include 10–15% contingency for survey findings.
  • A boat failing one tier's economics doesn't eliminate its make/model — the same model at a different all-in number may still fit.
  • For the near and mid-term tiers, weight boat selection toward the Chesapeake Bay cruising profile: shoal draft (under 5 ft preferred, under 3 ft ideal) and either heavy displacement or multihull stability to handle the Bay's short steep chop. A boat that can't comfortably use the Bay's shallow anchorages loses most of the near/mid-term value of the location.

Quality bar

Research outputs should be specific enough to act on — not "insurance is complicated" but "Markel requires ASA 104 or equivalent for offshore coverage on vessels over 30ft." Vague summaries are not useful here. Boat recommendations must be tied to a specific tier and include the reasoning for that placement.

Hard rules

  • Never recommend a boat that hasn't been checked against known class issues and insurance requirements.
  • Never compare boats on sticker price alone — use all-in cost.
  • Never dismiss a make/model based on a single bad example. Record the specific boat's issues, not a category rejection.
